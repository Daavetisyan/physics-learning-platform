# Lesson Production Standard

Every public lesson must pass this standard before it is marked **production**.

## 1. Academic purpose

Each lesson must define:

- A precise essential question
- 3–5 measurable learning objectives
- Required prerequisite knowledge
- The lesson's place in the course progression
- A realistic completion-time range

A lesson is not a topic summary. It must build a connected mental model and prepare students for the next lesson.

## 2. Theory depth

The theory section must:

- Develop the concept in logically ordered chapters
- Explain why the idea is needed before introducing notation
- Define every new term before using it heavily
- Connect ordinary language with precise physics language
- Explain signs, units, diagrams, and assumptions explicitly
- Include conceptual, mathematical, and real-world interpretations where relevant
- Distinguish the physical event from the model used to describe it
- Address limiting cases and changes of viewpoint when relevant
- Avoid unexplained formulas and unexplained vocabulary

Target depth depends on the topic, but a normal lesson should contain several substantial theory chapters rather than short summary cards.

## 3. Active understanding

Theory must be interrupted by short checks that diagnose misconceptions. Every checkpoint needs:

- One focused question
- Plausible distractors based on common mistakes
- Immediate explanatory feedback
- A clear connection to the preceding theory

Students should make predictions before seeing important simulation results or demonstrations.

## 4. Vocabulary

Every new technical term needs:

- A student-readable definition
- A concrete example
- A distinction from similar terms
- Consistent use throughout the lesson

## 5. Visual and video design

Every planned video must have:

- A learning purpose not already satisfied by text
- A storyboard
- Physical or visual demonstrations
- Prediction pauses
- On-screen labels for units, axes, directions, and reference frames
- A target duration
- Filming and editing notes

Videos reinforce the lesson; they do not replace the theoretical explanation.

## 6. Simulation or interactive investigation

A simulation is included only when changing variables helps students discover a relationship. It must include:

- A scientific question
- Controllable variables
- Clearly displayed outputs with units
- A prediction before experimentation
- An observation guide
- Interpretation questions
- Correct behavior for edge cases

A moving animation without a thinking task does not qualify as a simulation.

## 7. Worked examples

Worked examples must show:

- How the situation is interpreted
- What information is known
- What reference system or assumptions are used
- Why a particular relationship applies
- Substitution with units
- Calculation or logical reasoning
- Interpretation of signs and final results
- A statement explaining why the method works

Examples should progress from direct application to transfer and conceptual reasoning.

## 8. Misconceptions

Every lesson must explicitly address likely errors. Each misconception needs:

- The incorrect idea in recognizable student language
- The corrected idea
- An explanation of why the incorrect idea fails
- A way for the student to test or visualize the correction when possible

## 9. Practice progression

Practice should normally contain three levels:

- **Foundation:** vocabulary, diagrams, signs, units, and direct application
- **Standard:** calculations and multi-representation reasoning
- **Challenge:** transfer, justification, model choice, and unfamiliar situations

Students should be asked to explain answers, not only provide numbers.

## 10. Assessment

A mastery assessment must sample the full lesson, not only formula substitution. It should include:

- Concept definitions
- Diagram or coordinate interpretation
- Calculation where appropriate
- Units and signs
- Application to a new situation
- A frame-of-reference or assumption check when relevant

The default mastery threshold is 75%. Missed questions must point students toward the relevant theory.

## 11. Homework

Homework must extend the lesson rather than duplicate the quiz. It should include one or more of:

- A real-world observation
- Measurement or data collection
- A written explanation
- A diagram or model
- Multi-step reasoning
- Reflection on assumptions or limitations

## 12. AI scientist behavior

The AI character must be grounded in the lesson content and must:

- Explain at the student's grade level
- Ask guiding questions in Guide mode
- Check reasoning, units, signs, and interpretations in Check mode
- Avoid pretending to be the real historical scientist
- Avoid inventing historical quotations
- Encourage an attempt before revealing complete homework answers
- Refer the student back to exact lesson sections when needed

## 13. Accessibility and usability

Every lesson must support:

- Keyboard-accessible controls
- Clear headings and section navigation
- Readable contrast and scalable text
- Reduced-motion preferences
- Explicit labels rather than color-only meaning
- Mobile and desktop layouts
- Voice input only as an optional enhancement

## 14. Production quality gate

A lesson remains **draft** until:

1. Physics content is reviewed by both course creators.
2. All examples and assessment answers are independently checked.
3. The simulation is tested across its complete input range.
4. A grade 7, grade 8, and grade 9 student each complete the lesson.
5. Confusing wording and navigation points are recorded and corrected.
6. The AI assistant is tested against common misconceptions and unsafe prompt behavior.
7. Automated platform tests pass.
